# Hold Your Ideas: The 2027 Civic Interior, with Hiroshi Tanaka

*How Hiroshi Tanaka, senior designer on Honda's interior-design team in Tokyo, used Vizcom to take the 2027 Civic interior redesign from sketch to executive review in two days instead of seven.*

![2027 Honda Civic interior concept, rendered in Vizcom](samples/renders/honda_civic_interior.png)

## The Spark

Hiroshi Tanaka is a senior designer on Honda's interior-design team in Tokyo. He has spent the last nine months bringing Vizcom into the early phase of the 2027 Civic interior redesign — the kind of program where every fabric and every proportion is watched by the executive design team long before any of it reaches a clay model.

The work did not start with a tool decision. It started with a familiar problem: the gap between the moment a designer can see the interior in their head and the moment the executive design team can see it in a render. That gap, at most teams of comparable scale, is measured in weeks. On the 2027 Civic, Hiroshi's team set out to measure it in days.

> "Vizcom collapses the gap between sketch and review. We're presenting to the executive design team with renders that used to take a week. The decision velocity has changed how we work."
> — Hiroshi Tanaka, Senior Designer, Honda

## Rethinking the Interior Review Cycle

The old shape of an interior concept review — and not just at Honda — is well known. The designer sketches. The sketches are sent out to a visualization partner. The partner returns work in roughly three weeks. The stakeholders gather. The cycle restarts when something needs to move.

Under that cadence Hiroshi's team ran around three design reviews per quarter. With Vizcom in the workflow, they now run around eleven. Concept-to-review time has compressed from seven days to two.

The number that matters is not eleven. The number that matters is that a question raised in the morning meeting can be answered, visually, in the afternoon — by the designer who raised it.

## Hiroshi's Vizcom Workflow

### Studio — early ideation, full-bleed

Hiroshi's team uses Studio for the first pass on the concept. A full-bleed canvas with no template constraints lets the proportion question land on the page before anyone has decided what kind of render is coming next. The early ideas are allowed to stay loose. That looseness is the point — it is where the interior is found, not where it is dressed.

### Modify — material exploration at the speed of the conversation

Interior design lives or dies on material. On the 2027 Civic the team has tested more than thirty interior fabric combinations using Modify, without re-prompting and without leaving the canvas. The point is not that thirty is a large number. The point is that a material question raised in a review can be answered in the same review, by the same designer, in front of the same stakeholders.

### Animate — stakeholder walkthroughs without the pre-vis cycle

The largest compression sits here. The interior-design team used to send concept reviews out for a three-week pre-visualization cycle before walking the executive design team through a candidate interior. Animate replaces that cycle. The designer who built the concept is the designer who presents it — and the executive team sees the interior at the cadence the program needs, not at the cadence external vendor pipelines allow.

## From Pixels to the Executive Design Room

What the numbers describe — three quarterly reviews moving to eleven, seven days moving to two — is not a productivity claim. It is a description of who is in the room, how often, and with what fidelity behind them. Decisions are getting made by the people who made the sketches.

Hiroshi calls it decision velocity. It is the right phrase. The work has not gotten faster in the way a press release would frame it. The work has gotten closer — closer to the designer's hands, closer to the executive team's eyes, closer in time between the two.

## A Note on Finding the Right Moment for AI

The 2027 Civic is the kind of program where any tool has to earn its place against a stack validated over decades. The reason a team like Hiroshi's runs Vizcom across nine months and three connected features is not that the renders are pretty. It is that the renders arrive in time to matter.

That is the only test that ever counted for industrial design tools. The good ones move at the speed of the conversation. On this program, Vizcom has done that.
