# Hold Your Ideas: Velocity, Maya Okonkwo's Cast-Bronze Skateboard

How Maya Okonkwo used Vizcom to live with a surface for two weeks before sending files to the foundry.

![Velocity hero render — cast-bronze skateboard by Maya Okonkwo](samples/renders/velocity_hero.png)

## The Spark

Maya Okonkwo, an industrial designer based in Lagos (@mayaokonkwo.design), wanted to capture the moment a wave breaks. Not a picture of it — the object that holds it. She landed on a skateboard. A skateboard is one of the few everyday objects you stand on. Everyday objects make the strangest carriers for the sublime, and that contradiction is the project.

The result is Velocity: a single-edition skateboard with a deck shaped from one piece of cast bronze, the grip surface micro-textured to read as rippled water. The board weighs 11.4 kg. It is not built to skate. It is built to be present.

## Rethinking the Skateboard

A skateboard is usually subtracted from — sanded plywood, screen-printed graphic, the lightest possible thing it can be. Velocity adds. The deck is a bronze casting. The trucks are brushed steel, custom. Only the wheels are off-the-shelf — polyurethane, 56mm, 78A — because the wheel is one of the few parts that has nothing to do with this story.

Bronze does what plywood cannot. It catches light at depth. It holds patina. Cast it, and a flat surface stops being flat — there is a slow undulation across the deck that reads like water seconds before the break. That's the texture Maya was after, and bronze was the only honest way to get there.

## Material Choices: A Single Piece of Bronze

The deck is one piece of bronze. Not laminated, not panelled. The whole-piece constraint forced every decision about the surface to happen before the casting — there is no second pass on a single-edition cast. Maya spent her design time where it mattered: in the texture.

The micro-pattern that reads as rippled water is not stamped or sanded after the fact. It is in the wax. The foundry in Accra cast it through, lost-wax, with the surface already decided.

## Maya's Vizcom Workflow

### Rapid surface exploration in Modify

Maya started in Vizcom Workbench with a CAD line drawing brought in from Rhino. The form was settled — the skateboard shape — but the surface was open. She used Modify to test roughly twenty different surface-texture variations in a single afternoon, walking the deck through versions that ran from glassy and still to choppy and broken.

Twenty variations in an afternoon is the number that mattered. The deck was going to be cast once. The texture had to be right the first time, which meant seeing the wrong ones — fast — until the right one was undeniable.

### Setting patina in Instant Render

Once the surface read correctly, Maya moved to Instant Render to set the bronze patina. Patina is a material decision, not a styling one. It changes what the surface does with light, and that changes what the rippled-water texture says. She rendered the deck under daylight and under a single tungsten source until the patina sat where she wanted it.

Then she animated the final piece in Vizcom for her portfolio — a slow rotation under shifting light, the surface doing exactly what bronze does and water also does.

> "Vizcom let me make decisions about texture I would have had to leave to chance. By the time I sent files to the foundry, I had already lived with the surface for two weeks."
> — Maya Okonkwo

## From Vizcom to Bronze

Maya partnered with a foundry in Accra to lost-wax cast the bronze deck. The handoff was clean because the surface was already specified — the wax already carried the rippled-water micro-pattern, and the patina decision was made before the metal was hot.

Total weight: 11.4 kg. Heavy on purpose. Velocity is not about skatepark performance. It is about presence — the way an object that should be light becomes an object that has to be carried with two hands. A skateboard that asks you to stop is a strange thing. That is the point.

## A Note to Fellow Designers

The middle phase of a project is where most ideas thin out. You commit to a form, you commit to a material, and then there is a long, expensive stretch in which surface decisions get made by tooling rather than by you. Single-edition pieces punish that stretch — there is no version two.

Vizcom does not remove the casting. It does not pour the bronze. What it does is let the surface decisions happen before any of that — at the speed of the eye, not the speed of a foundry schedule. Twenty variations in an afternoon is what makes a single-edition piece honest. You see the wrong ones. You discard them. You are not guessing when you ship.
